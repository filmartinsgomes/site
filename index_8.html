About

Strip away the jargon and innovation comes down to something simple: organisations don't learn, spot opportunities, or create value on their own — people do that work. Knowledge gets generated, shared, and absorbed by human beings; dynamic capabilities live in them, not in the org chart. I believe the people and bodies that connect all of this — the intermediaries, whether a whole organisation or a single individual brokering across boundaries — are the most important and most overlooked part of how innovation actually happens.

That conviction matters more now, not less — though not in the way the headlines suggest. Part of what makes us human is our capacity to imagine, build, and wield tools; AI is the newest of them, not an exception to them. The question was never people or technology — it's that the toolmaker matters more than the tool. So as AI, automation and digitalisation reshape how organisations work, value doesn't drain away from people; it concentrates in them — in the judgement, the translation, and the trust that decide whether a new capability actually takes hold.

This isn't abstract for me. For nearly two decades I've designed and delivered innovation and growth programmes across deep tech and venture building — in the UK, Brazil and the Middle East — and today I do it as a Principal Innovation Solutions Consultant at Digital Catapult, itself an innovation intermediary. Alongside practice, I'm completing a PhD on innovation intermediaries in resource-constrained ecosystems. I write to close the distance between that thinking and what a practitioner can actually do on Monday morning — connecting dynamic capabilities, innovation management and organisational learning, with people at the centre of all three.
